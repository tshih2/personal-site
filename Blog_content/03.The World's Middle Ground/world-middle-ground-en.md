# The World's Middle Ground

This morning I read an article about cars and it made me notice something: most vehicles on the market these days seem to be hybrids.

Not long ago, the market was still arguing over whether electric cars would completely replace gas ones. Back then it really felt like EVs were about to take over the world. But here we are, and it turns out the middle ground is where things actually landed. That got me wondering whether the same logic applies to AI, which is moving at a speed nothing else in tech ever has.

### 1. Take away the scarcity, and the fun goes with it

Every day there's some new take about AI replacing human jobs, and even Elon Musk is pushing AI chips to handle internal work. A lot of people are picturing a kind of utopia: buy one AI, let it make money for you, and just lie around at home playing games and eating and sleeping on repeat.

Honestly, I can't picture actually wanting that life. **Play gets old fast.** That's basically what "scarcity makes things valuable" means. Work might not be fun, but it's exactly because work exists that your days off and your rest actually mean something. Take scarcity away entirely, and life loses its value along with it. The middle ground, for a human life, sits somewhere between working yourself into the ground and having zero direction while you play.

### 2. What AI still can't touch: feeling and empathy

AI is impressive, but its weak spots are obvious too. It loses track of context all the time, and sometimes that leads it to do something harmful just because it misread what you meant. The bigger issue is that **AI doesn't feel anything.**

That's part of why I believe my own field, UX, still matters. We can design good products because we have empathy, because we can put ourselves in someone else's shoes. That kind of emotional read between two people isn't something current technology can replace.

### 3. Things that were declared dead and never actually died

Looking back, plenty of new technologies have come with predictions that some industry was finished. Almost every time, things settled into a middle ground instead.

Retail is one example: e-commerce versus physical stores. When Amazon and Taobao took off, everyone said brick-and-mortar was finished. But it turns out shopping in person was never really about convenience. It's about **the warmth of interacting with another human being.** We're social animals, and we depend on that kind of connection for a lot of our happiness.

Reading is another: e-books versus print. Kindle was everywhere when it launched, but print books are still standing. There's something about the physical feeling of turning a page that gives you a real sense of having absorbed something, in a way that scrolling on a phone or tablet never quite delivers.

Finance too: crypto versus traditional banking. Even as crypto tries to replace the old system, companies like Visa and Mastercard keep partnering with crypto firms instead of fighting them. Not everyone is willing to trust code alone with their money. People who don't feel secure with that still want an actual institution holding it for them.

### Conclusion: giving up on absolutes is what lets you see the whole picture

Nothing in this world seems to be truly absolute. It's a lot like a relationship: two people argue, and eventually they sit down and land on some middle ground, some compromise they can both live with.

Instead of worrying about who's going to replace who, it's probably more useful to think about where the balance actually sits. **Not chasing extremes, but trying to see the whole picture,** that's where I think the real opportunity is.
